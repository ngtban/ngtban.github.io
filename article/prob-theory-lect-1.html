4 σ\sigmaσ-FIELDS

Proposition 1: Any intersection of sigma algebras over the same sample space is itself a sigma algebra.

Ideally, we would like to specify the probability P(A)P(A)P(A) of every subset of Ω\OmegaΩ. However, if we wish our probabilities to have certain intuitive mathematicalproperties, we run into some insurmountable mathematical difficulties. A solution is provided by the following compromise: assign probabilities to only a partial collection of subsets of Ω\OmegaΩ. The sets in this collection are to be thought of as the “nice” subsets of Ω\OmegaΩ, or, alternatively, as the subsets of of interest. Mathematically, we will require this collection to be a σ\sigmaσ-field a term that we define next

4.1 Other reasons for “small” σ\sigmaσ-fields.

As we discussed earlier, one reason for using a σ-field which does not include all subsets of is in order to avoid insurmountable mathematical difficulties. However, there is also another reason: we may want to capture the perspective of an observer who receives only partial information about the outcome of the experiment. In that case, it is convenient (loosely speaking) to let F be just the set of events for which the observer will be able to tell whether they occurred or not.

We will turn to this association of σ\sigmaσ-fields to observers much later, when we consider conditional expectations given partial information.
